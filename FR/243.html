A month later, Aurora was about to head back to the Institute, ready to get back on that next-level research with her professors.

Before she left, the Barron and Hawke families came together to celebrate her engagement to Julian. The ceremony was a whirlwind of joy, sealing their bond with promises of a shared future.

Shortly after, Aurora made an unexpected detour. She went to prison.

She hadn't bothered visiting Joshua, Bertha, and the others since they got thrown behind bars.

But now, with her Institute move coming up and no clue when she'd be back, she figured it was time to face them.

This, she sensed, might be their final encounter. After all, they'd all been sentenced to death.

Before their executions, she wanted to see her enemies brought low, to honor the Aurora of her past life, who'd suffered so much at their hands.

*****

In the prison's stark visiting room, Aurora first came face-to-face with Joshua.

She'd braced herself for his hatred, expecting eyes that burned with venom, yearning to tear her apart.

The man behind the thick glass looked like he'd aged decades overnight. However, his gaze, to her shock, carried not rage but guilt.

There was something else in his eyes, a flicker of paternal warmth. It was as if he were a father looking at his daughter.

Aurora narrowed her eyes, a suspicion taking root.

She picked up the phone, and Joshua mirrored her, his trembling hand clutching the receiver.

Before she could speak, his voice, raw and unsteady, crackled through.

"Aurora, I'm so sorry. It's all my fault. I never should've hurt you for Isabella and her wretched daughter Bertha.

"I know I was wrong. Can you ever forgive me?"

Aurora let out a sharp, mirthless laugh at his heartfelt plea. "So that's it. If I'm not mistaken, Joshua, you've remembered our past life, haven't you?"

Joshua's face crumpled, heavy with remorse.

Since his imprisonment, he'd endured daily beatings, and with each blow, memories of his past life had surfaced.

Now he understood the agony Aurora had suffered in prison, tormented and broken.

He cursed himself for not remembering that sooner, for not realizing the truth the moment Aurora was reborn.

He hated that it took a jail cell to open his eyes.

Now, it was all too late. Condemned to death, he could never make amends.

Why did fate let me remember... Just to punish me?

Tears of regret streamed down his face as he looked at Aurora.

"I know my sins are terrible. I don't ask for much... Just your forgiveness. Please, Aurora, can you give me that?"

Her gaze was ice-cold, unyielding. "Forgive you? You don't deserve it.

"I'm kinder than you ever were, Joshua. Here I am, visiting you in prison. In my past life, you threw me into a cell for five years, and not even one of you ever came to see me.

"Compared to that, I'm practically a saint."

Her mocking words pierced Joshua's heart, each one a fresh wound.

"Can't you forgive me, just this once?" he pleaded. "I'm about to die."

"Death is what you earned," Aurora shot back, her eyes glinting with scorn. "What, you think you can guilt me into pity at the last second?

"You made my life a living hell in our last life. I'd rather you paid for it forever. This is only your second life, so why should I forgive you now?

"I just dropped by to see you hit rock bottom, Joshua.

"And knowing you're getting the special treatment in here? That's enough for me.

"You used to tell me five years in prison was no big deal.

"So, go on, and experience it yourself. Not everyone gets to savor the taste of a cell.

"When you're done, you can face your end. And if there's another life after this, I'll still wish you nothing but suffering, forever."

Joshua's eyes widened, his body trembling as her merciless words sank in.

Aurora hated him.

He knew his crimes were monstrous, but in his past life, paralyzed by a stroke and abandoned in the wilderness, he'd endured torment he thought had balanced the scales.

He believed he'd paid his debt to Aurora.

He couldn't fathom why she still refused to let go.

This life, too, had cost him dearly. He was sentenced to death.

Surely, he believed, that was enough.

He didn't know why she cursed him to eternal suffering.

Sobbing, he begged, "Aurora, I've learned my lesson. Why can't you forgive me?

"I paid for my mistakes in our last life and this one. Isn't that enough?

"In the next life, I swear I'll be a proper father. Please, just believe me."

But Aurora's heart was stone.

Forgiveness?

She wouldn't give him that.

She wouldn't give him a chance to escape from the punishment he deserved.

To her, wounds didn't fade with time. They scarred and etched deep into her soul.

She would never forgive, not in this life or any other.

With a faint, bitter smile, she said, "Joshua, your word means zero to me.

"You used to say I was mean to Bertha because I was just born evil. Well, let me flip that back on you. Someone as messed up as you? You could live a hundred lives and still be trash. And trash like you deserves everything coming to it."

"No, I won't be," Joshua cried, desperation in his voice. "Aurora, I'm begging you, give me a chance."

But Aurora had already set the phone down. She turned and walked away, not sparing him another glance.

Joshua pounded on the glass, frantic, but she never looked back.

He never saw her again, not even before his execution.

As for Isabella, Frank, and Bertha, their days were a relentless cycle of abuse from fellow inmates.

Bertha, already weakened by kidney disease, didn't survive long enough for her execution.

She was beaten to death by prisoners, her ears deafened, her eyes blinded by needles, her limbs and ribs shattered, and her skull caved in.

She lay in a pool of blood, memories of her former life playing like a film reel in her broken mind.

Every time her family cursed or beat Aurora, Bertha just stood nearby, smug and triumphant, looking down on her like a queen watching a fallen servant.

The images fueled her bitterness.

She didn't want to die. She believed she was supposed to be the victor, the one adored and centered in everyone's world.

She couldn't understand how it had all gone so wrong.

But no amount of resentment could save her. Bertha died, consumed by regret.

Isabella and Frank, physically tougher, endured daily beatings right up until the eve of their executions.

Aurora checked on everybody who did her dirty except Barnett.

His fake self just made her sick, acting all nice while plotting against her.

Even looking at him felt like a stain on her soul.

So she just cut him off completely.

As Aurora stepped out of the prison, ready to climb into her car, she froze.

A group of people were being marched in by police, and at the front was a young man with garish yellow hair, dressed in cheap, flashy clothes.

He exuded the careless swagger of a street thug, the kind who lived for trouble.

Aurora's breath caught.

She knew that face all too well.
