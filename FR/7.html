Aurora gazed at Daisy, her heart twisting with bittersweet feelings. Out of everyone at Elvador Villa, Daisy alone had shown her real kindness, treating her like she belonged.

She remembered how Daisy had dipped into her own savings to buy her a fan when she'd broken out in rashes during those brutal summer months in the stuffy storage room. When winter's chill crept in, it was Daisy who'd quietly slipped her a hot water bottle to fight off the cold.

These memories brought tears to Aurora's eyes, the simple acts of kindness standing out like beacons against years of loneliness.

Daisy saw the injuries on Aurora's body.

"Your injuries...Ms. Elvador, let me take care of those injuries. "

"It's nothing—just a scratch," Aurora shrugged it off. "I'm used to this stuff."

Daisy's heart ached at those words. No one should be so accustomed to pain that they'd dismiss their injuries like this.

The next second, Daisy's eyes caught the plastic bag dangling from Aurora's hand. "Ms. Elvador... you're leaving, aren't you?" she asked softly.

Aurora stood there for a moment, struggling to find words before finally giving a simple nod.

"Yes, I'm out of here. "

Pain flickered across Daisy's features. She wanted to beg Aurora to stay, but the words died in her throat. After all, she'd witnessed firsthand how the Elvadors had treated Aurora—the constant rejection, the unfairness, the daily humiliations. She couldn't bring herself to ask Aurora to endure more of that.

"If you've made your decision, I won't try to change your mind," Daisy finally said with a heavy sigh. "But please, at least take this."

Daisy pulled out a bundle of cash, pushing it into Aurora's hands.

"Miss, The world out there is rough." Her voice cracked as tears started rolling down her cheeks. "Just... just promise me you'll stay safe."

Aurora bit her lip, trying to hold back tears but failing as they slid down her face.

It hit her hard—how this woman, who wasn't even family, showed more care than her actual relatives ever had. Her own parents and brother couldn't spare her a shred of kindness, yet here was Daisy, worried sick about her wellbeing.

*****

Barnett slumped in his car in the garage, eyes fixed on the dashcam screen. The footage showed Aurora's fifteen-minute ride home from prison—she might as well have been a statue. Just sat there, hands folded in her lap, head against the window, completely still.

The dress they'd accused her of stealing lay untouched. She hadn't even looked at it.

Guilt gnawed at his stomach as he remembered their accusations. The weight of it all felt suffocating. Those eyes of hers—once warm, now ice-cold—haunted him.

He remembered how she used to be—that bright smile whenever he came home, the way she'd call out "Welcome back!" and fuss over him like a mother hen. "You must be exhausted," she'd say, always making sure he had everything he needed after work.

Barnett watched her, feeling like he was staring at a stranger instead of his sister. The realization hit him like a truck, bringing a splitting headache. He slumped against the leather seat, shutting his eyes.

Time blurred until Aurora's voice cut through his thoughts. "Hey Daisy, I got it from here. Head back inside, okay?"

"Call if you need anything. Stay safe!"

Barnett's eyes flew open at their exchange. Through the car window, he spotted Aurora and Daisy chatting by the villa's entrance, their silhouettes clear against the building's facade.

"Take care, Daisy," Aurora turned to leave.

"Aurora! Where the hell do you think you're going?" Barnett burst out of the car, his voice shattering the quiet courtyard.

"I said stop right there."

Aurora kept limping forward like she hadn't heard a word. Her complete dismissal hit him like a punch to the gut. That's when it clicked—she was actually leaving. For good.

Fear gripped him as he rushed over, grabbing her arm.
