Chapter 38 Peril in the Cave

The karst cave hid behind a crashing waterfall, its roar hammering the rocks and swallowing the sound of their footsteps.

With one swift motion, Ciotti cut through the curtain of vines veiling the entrance. A damp chill, sharp with sulfur, rushed out and prickled their skin.

"Flare," she called, her voice echoing in the hollow dark.

Evander fired. The flare burst upward, flooding the cave in a harsh crimson glow. For a heartbeat, it was lit like midday.

Stalactites hung like jagged teeth, their wet surfaces gleaming. Scattered across the stone floor lay skeletal remains, twisted and still, their hollow sockets staring into the void.

"Someone got here before us," Evander muttered, lifting a tarnished tag from a ribcage. The engraved number was faint but legible. "Looks like one of the teams from fifty years ago."

Ciotti crouched beside a skull, her fingers brushing the bullet hole at its temple—gentle, almost reverent, like she was afraid to disturb something that still lingered.

Evander's eyes snagged on a silver bracelet glinting at the skeleton's wrist. It matched the one Ciotti wore.

"That's my grandfather," she said softly. "He was the last one to see the treasure."

The flare sputtered out, and the cave dropped into a sickly yellow gloom.

Ciotti cracked glow sticks, lining them across the floor. Pale green light spread in a thin belt, revealing ancient carvings snaking along the walls.

The symbols mirrored the Latin script on their map, arrows pointing deeper into the cave.

"He left us a trail," Ciotti whispered, running her fingers over one of the arrows. "They say the treasure's sealed in the Crimson Coffin… but it's trapped."

Evander's hand shot out, grabbing her wrist before she could trace farther.

At the glow's edge, a thin scar cut through the rock.

"That's no natural crack."

He scraped it with his dagger. Metal gleamed beneath the stone.

"It's an alloy. Trigger mechanism. One wrong move and we're done."

Before Evander could pull his hand back, a stench hit him—rank, coppery, thick. Like rot and rust brewed together. It clung to his throat, forcing him to hold his breath.

Ciotti reacted instantly. Her hand swept back, unclipping the submachine gun in one smooth motion. Steel caught the glow stick's light, flashing cold.

"Freeze," she whispered, her voice low and taut.

Her finger hovered on the trigger as she stared into the shadows.

Two hulking shapes emerged.

Their matted manes hung in filthy ropes, amber eyes burning with unnatural fire. Claws scraped loose stone, blood dripping fresh from their jaws.

Whatever they'd killed—it hadn't been long ago.

Evander flicked his gun to rapid-fire, his knuckles whitening around the grip.

They moved back-to-back, a tight circle of steel, barrels locked on the lions' chests.

The beasts padded closer. Evander saw flesh strung between their teeth, their hot, bloody breath steaming in the chill.

The lead lion growled—low, guttural—then raked the ground with its paw before launching forward like a shadow unchained.

Evander and Ciotti fired as one.

The cave erupted in thunder. Muzzles flashed, weaving a web of fire. Bullets tore into the lion, blood spraying across its fur. Its body faltered mid-lunge, crashing down with a bone-jarring thud. Its amber eyes dimmed, a last whimper rattling out before stillness.

The second lion froze, tail lashing. For a heartbeat, Evander thought it would charge—then it spun, vanishing into the cave's black abyss. Its claws clattered on stone, then faded into silence.

Ciotti's eyes narrowed. She drew her sidearm and strode to the fallen beast. Its chest heaved weakly, breaths shallow and ragged.

Without hesitation, she pressed the barrel to its head and fired three times.

The muffled cracks echoed through the chamber. Blood sprayed, streaking her camo pants like dark blossoms.

She lowered the gun, eyes cold as frost.

"Mercy to a beast is a knife in your own back."

Evander said nothing. He only scanned the darkness, his grip tight on his weapon. The glow stick's sickly light barely cut ten feet, the black beyond pulsing as if unseen eyes watched from the walls.

"Move," Ciotti ordered, holstering her pistol and hefting the submachine gun. "Keep your distance. Watch the ceiling."

They crept deeper, each step deliberate, their silence broken only by the crunch of grit underfoot.

Evander held the glow stick high. Its green light danced over the stalactites above—jagged, twisted shapes that hung like jaws waiting to snap shut.

The floor was a graveyard. Bones littered the path, some frozen mid-flight as if they'd died running, spines twisted into grotesque arches of agony.

"The traps aren't just on the ground," Evander said, nudging a skeleton aside with his boot. Beneath it, a metal plate glinted faintly in the rock. "See the rust? Old spring triggers. Crossbow bolts."

Ciotti crouched, pouring liquid from a small vial onto the plate. White foam hissed up instantly.

"Zinc-copper alloy. Heat sets it off."

She pried loose a stone, revealing a taut wire beneath. Her jaw tightened.

"My father's journal warned me—these traps are chained."

Before she could rise, Evander yanked her back hard by the shoulder.

A whoosh tore through the air.

Three bolts streaked past, grazing her scalp and burying themselves in the ground with a vicious hum. Black venom dripped from their tips, sizzling holes into the rock.

Ciotti's breath caught, sweat sliding cold down her spine. "Thanks."

She glanced upward. Metallic glints winked among the stalactites.

"Traps up there too."

Their eyes met. Without a word, Evander pulled climbing rope from his pack, tied one end around his waist, and passed the other to her.

"Stay fifteen feet back. I'll take point."

Step by step, he advanced, testing each stone with his knife before putting his weight down. Ciotti followed, eyes flicking between walls and ceiling, finger resting lightly on her trigger.

Halfway through, Evander froze.

The glow lit a patch of darker stone ahead, its edges etched with faint seams. He tapped it with his dagger—hollow.

"Don't step there. It's a drop."

Ciotti swept her detector across it. Red dots bloomed across the screen.

"Spikes below. One slip and…" She didn't finish. She didn't need to.

Evander scanned the walls and pointed at a narrow crevice to the left.

"There. We can squeeze through."

The crack was barely wide enough for one. Evander slid in first, shoulders scraping the stone, Ciotti following close. Their breaths and heartbeats tangled in the tight dark, sulfur thick in the air. Droplets tapped on their helmets like tiny hammers.

Then, abruptly, the passage widened.

They stepped out into a vast stone chamber.

At its center rested a coffin the color of blood, gem-encrusted and glowing faintly in the pale light. The Crimson Coffin—just as the map had promised.

But in the far corner, something moved.

A black shape dragged itself from the shadows—the lion that had escaped earlier.

Its hind leg hung twisted, its growl low and ragged. It bared its teeth, but it was too weak to rise.
