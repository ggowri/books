Chapter 42 Fall

Ciotti glanced back at Evander, sweat glinting on her brow in the eerie blue light.

"The chains can take it. Let them cross," she said.

Evander hollered across the bridge. Madden stepped up first, his safety lock clinking against the steel. Alex and Dylan followed close behind, their nervous faces slowly giving way to excitement. Dylan even whistled, flashing Ciotti a thumbs-up.

Their packs were light—just rations and tools. No parachutes.

"One at a time!" Evander called, his hand brushing the lock at his waist. The cold metal steadied him.

The bridge still swayed gently, humming from the aftershock of the wind.

Madden stepped onto the chain, spreading his arms for balance. His boots creaked on the rusted steel, the safety lock sliding with a steady rhythm.

"Alex, go!" Evander shouted, his voice echoing in the abyss.

Alex climbed on. Dylan followed right behind. They moved like insects on a thread, fifteen feet apart, swaying side to side.

Evander and Ciotti waited at the midpoint, flashlights tracking every step.

Then the wind came.

A sharper gust roared up from the abyss, ice-cold, laced with grit that stung like sandpaper. The bridge tilted violently. Dylan screamed, slamming into Alex. The two tangled on the chains.

"Hold on!" Evander's shout was swallowed by the gale.

Madden turned, trying to pull Alex up. All three bodies pressed down on the bridge's center. The chains groaned.

Then came the crack.

It rang sharp and final, like ice shattering. Evander's eyes shot wide as the far-side anchor gave way. The chain snapped, jagged brown metal tearing open like a wound.

"Damn it!" Ciotti lunged, fingertips grazing Madden's strap—too far.

The bridge bucked like a severed serpent and collapsed into the abyss.

Weightlessness hit Evander like a punch. Wind howled in his ears as he tumbled. He caught Ciotti's wrist, bodies colliding midair.

Their world spun, filled with the desperate screams of Madden and the others—screams that broke into raw despair before the thunder of chains slamming stone drowned them out.

"Parachutes!" Ciotti's shout cut through the chaos. She yanked her cord.

Evander fumbled, fingers finding the ring. The chute burst open, yanking him hard, jerking them upward with brutal force. White canvas bloomed against the dim blue glow like a ghostly flower.

Ciotti's grip crushed his wrist as they swung, cords groaning, speed slowing at last.

"Don't—" she started, but Evander had already looked down.

His stomach lurched. He caught the glimpse anyway.

Madden and the others fell like broken kites, limbs flailing, no parachutes to save them. Dylan's flashlight slipped, beam tumbling until it struck a jagged rock with a dull crack and went dark.

Then the crashes came—two sickening thuds, heavy as hammers into wet clay, echoing up the walls. Blood sprayed in the glow, scattering like red fireworks before being swallowed by the green mist below.

Madden's last look, burned into Evander's vision—no anger, no fight, only a hollow kind of relief.

"Stop looking." Ciotti's hand covered his eyes. Her palm was cold, trembling.

Evander gently pulled it away. His throat burned, words stuck like a stone. Madden's earlier words came back: Their leader's markings end here. Now he understood what that meant.

The parachute dragged them sideways, wind slamming them toward a rock wall. Ciotti slashed two cords with her dagger, steering them toward a narrow crevice. The chute snagged vines, rustling as they dropped onto the rocks below.

"Check gear," she ordered, voice flat.

Evander nodded, fingers brushing his pistol, rifling through his pack. Most equipment survived, though the compass needle spun wildly.

Ciotti shone her beam upward. "We're two hundred, maybe two-fifty feet down." The broken bridge above was only a faint outline in the mist.

Evander stepped to the edge of the crevice. Green flashes stirred in the abyss. He couldn't tell if they were bird eyes or the shattered remnants of his fallen companions. Dylan's whistle, Madden's confident grin—they clawed at his chest.

"They shouldn't have come," he muttered, guilt dragging at his voice. If he'd pushed harder to leave them behind, maybe…

Ciotti gave a short, cold laugh, stabbing her dagger into the dirt.

"In Mortis Gorge, it's always life or death. No in-between."

They stood in silence, the wind whispering through the cracks.

Ten minutes later, Ciotti crouched. Her light caught an arrow etched into the ground, pointing deeper into the crevice.

"My grandpa was here," she murmured, tracing the mark.

Evander blinked. The carving was fresh—no older than half a year.

His pulse quickened. "You mean—"

"Either he's still alive," Ciotti said, straightening, her eyes sharp as blades, "or someone's copying his trail. Either way, it's worse than we thought."
