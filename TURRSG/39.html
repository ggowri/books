Chapter 40 The Underworld

Evander and Ciotti's expressions hardened.

"If you've got what it takes, let us come with you," Madden said, eyes burning with hope. "We've been dying to crack the secrets of that maze—and the treasure waiting inside."

Ciotti glanced at Evander, searching his face.

He weighed it for a beat, then gave a short nod.

"Fine. But if you try anything—"

"No chance," Madden cut in, hands raised, tone earnest.

"You'd wipe the floor with us. We're not that dumb."

He barked a few words in a local dialect. His companions slung their golden axes over their shoulders, offering lopsided, almost sheepish grins.

"This is Alex McKinney," he said, nodding left. "And Dylan Willis," he added, pointing to the other man.

"Solid guys. Experts at hunting and tracking."

The two dipped their heads in silent greeting.

"So?" Madden rubbed his hands, eyes sparking. "We moving out?"

"Not yet," Evander said, stepping toward the Crimson Coffin. His gaze stayed fixed on its shadowy interior.

"What about what's inside?"

"Forget it," Madden muttered, clapping a heavy hand on his shoulder. Regret weighed down his voice.

"It's bait. The real prize is deeper. Our leader took the coffin once—triggered the first trap. That's how we lost him."

Evander hesitated, then waved everyone back.

"Clear out."

He gripped the coffin lid and heaved. Stone groaned, muscles straining as the slab shifted open.

A rush of icy air burst out, laced with that intoxicating spice-scent, thick and strange.

But instead of treasure, the coffin's base revealed a gaping black hole. A bottomless well.

Cold gusts rose from it, carrying the damp tang of earth. A shiver rippled through the group.

"So it really is an entrance," Ciotti murmured, leaning closer, excitement flickering in her eyes.

"I'll grab the ropes and parachute packs," Evander said. "Wait here."

"We're coming with you," Madden insisted. Alex and Dylan nodded in unison.

"I'll hold the fort," Ciotti said firmly, crouching by the hole to study its jagged rim.

"Don't worry—I'll be fine."

Evander glanced at her. In the weak glow, her profile was calm, unflinching, as if this abyss was nothing more than another challenge on her list. He trusted her.

"Watch yourself," he said, before heading out with Madden and the others.

Ciotti lingered in the chamber. She cracked a glow stick and dropped it into the pit. The green light spiraled down, spinning and shrinking until it disappeared completely. The shaft was deep—far deeper than she could see.

She scraped her dagger across the rock near the coffin. Faint carvings showed beneath the blade—marks left by others long ago.

Half an hour later, Evander and the others returned with ropes, parachute packs, and long-range flashlights.

"We're ready," Evander said, securing the rope to a stalactite.

"Check your gear. If it's good, we're going down."

Ciotti stood, slinging on a pack Evander handed her.

"I'm first."

"No—I know the ground down there," Madden cut in.

Ciotti's voice was iron.

"I'm going. I've got more experience than all of you. And I know the packs better. If the rope fails, I've got a backup."

Evander met her eyes. She was right. Her skill outshone them all.

"Be careful," he said quietly. "We'll cover you."

She flashed a grin, clipped the flashlight to her belt, and slipped into the well.

Evander and Madden held the rope, lowering her carefully.

"Slow," Ciotti's voice echoed up. "Walls are slick. Rocks jutting out."

They fed the rope down, eyes on the depth markers. Ciotti's flashlight danced against stone, then vanished into the black.

Twenty minutes later, she called up.

"Almost there. A chamber's opening."

The rope slackened. Her voice rose again, tinged with awe.

"I'm down. Safe. You need to see this."

Evander and Madden exchanged a relieved glance.

"Scout it first," Evander called.

"Already on it."

Moments later, her voice came again, alive with wonder.

"It's clear. Get down here. This place is unreal."

A spark of curiosity ignited in Evander's chest.

"I'm next."

He strapped on a pack, grabbed a flashlight, and signaled the others to hold steady. The descent was smoother than expected, the walls slick but manageable.

At a hundred feet, he saw Ciotti's beam flicker beyond a bend.

He rounded the corner—then froze.

The underground chamber spread out before him like another world. Rock walls shimmered faint blue, as though ghostly fire burned inside them. The glow bathed everything in an unearthly calm.

In the distance, jagged spires rose from the ground. Strange plants pulsed faintly with light, casting shifting patterns across the stone. The air smelled damp, earthy, threaded with a sweet, alien floral note.

Ciotti stood in the glow, flashlight sweeping the expanse, her face lit with awe.
