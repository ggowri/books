Chapter 118 Country Life

Morning light filtered through the mist and spilled across the Seine. Evander stirred at the sound of waterbirds calling overhead.

As he slowly opened his eyes, the first thing he saw was a stretch of cloudless blue sky.

The boat was no longer drifting. It sat still and steady, no longer bobbing with the current like it had the night before.

He sat up and realized the boat had floated into a tranquil, glassy lake.

This wasn't the main course of the Seine. The water was ringed by low, charming houses—red roofs, whitewashed walls, and ivy crawling up the facades. The air carried the warm scent of bread and fresh coffee.

He'd landed in a picture-perfect French village.

Evander stepped out of the boat and onto the grassy bank. Dewy blades of grass soaked into the soles of his shoes.

Lifting his head, he took in the unfamiliar town.

Each house was painted in cheerful colors—gold like melted butter, soft yellows that glowed like sunlight, reds as ripe as berries. Some had kept the original brickwork exposed, paired with white window frames and baskets of flowers. It all looked like a living oil painting.

At the center of the village stood a tall building with a sharp steeple crowned by a cross—clearly a church. Its pointed roof caught the morning sun, casting a gentle gleam, the most eye-catching mark in the entire town.

He followed the cobbled path along the lake, taking his time.

As he passed a bakery, the owner opened the door and greeted him cheerfully in French. He didn't catch the words, but her warmth needed no translation.

About half an hour later, he came to a quiet cottage on the edge of the village.

Flowers bloomed across the yard in vibrant yet understated hues, and on the front gate hung a hand-painted wooden sign that read "Maison de Fleurs"—a name as gentle and charming as the place itself.

Evander knocked on the door, and an elderly woman with snowy hair opened it, Madeleine.

Using clumsy but earnest French, he motioned that he wanted to rent the entire guesthouse.

Madeleine nodded with a smile, handed him a set of keys, and then gestured enthusiastically toward the nearby market while saying something about "fresh fish" and "just-picked vegetables".

Once inside, Evander finally pulled out his phone.

After drifting off course the night before, he'd lost all signal. But now, the bars jumped back to full.

He scrolled to "Sarah" in his contacts and hit the call button.

It rang three times before she answered, her voice edged with urgency.

"Mr. Bexley! Are you okay? I was worried sick last night. When I didn't hear from you this morning, I nearly called the police."

"I'm fine," Evander replied, voice rough with sleep but more at ease than it had been the night before.

"The boat drifted to a small town. It's safe here. Have you ever heard of Giverny?"

"Giverny?"

Sarah paused and then lit up.

"I know it! That's a famous village just outside Paris, known for Monet's garden and those endless fields of flowers. I actually did a countryside photo shoot nearby once! Wait for me, I'm driving over now. Should take me about two hours."

After the call, Evander followed the path Madeleine had pointed out, heading toward the village market.

The place bustled with life. Women in floral dresses carried woven baskets as they picked through vegetables. Fishermen displayed wriggling bass fresh from the lake. Bright red strawberries glistened in their crates, filling the air with sweetness.

He bought a whole sea bass, still kicking in the net. He added broccoli, tomatoes, and a box of strawberries, and then swung by the bakery for a bag of warm baguettes fresh from the oven.

With both arms full of ingredients, he walked back to Maison de Fleurs.

The moment he stepped inside, the cottage matched his expectations perfectly. Polished wooden floors, a cozy fireplace in the living room, and a small but well-stocked kitchen gave the place an unmistakable charm.

He tied on an apron and got to work on lunch.

The sea bass was cleaned and pan-seared in butter until golden and then sprinkled with thyme and a squeeze of lemon.

The broccoli and tomatoes were blanched and tossed in olive oil and balsamic vinegar.

He sliced the baguette, toasted it until crisp, and plated it alongside the strawberries.

Just as he finished setting the last dish on the table, he heard a car pull up outside, followed by hurried footsteps.

Sarah burst through the door, eyes landing on Evander beside the table, and all the tension she'd held in overnight collapsed in an instant.

She crossed the room in three quick steps and threw her arms around him, not caring that he was still holding a fork.

She hadn't slept a wink since returning to Paris. Every half hour, she had checked her phone, dreading the worst. But now, seeing him alive, safe, and somehow cooking lunch, all the fear and worry melted into sheer relief.

Evander froze, startled by the sudden embrace. The fork nearly slipped from his hand.

He could feel her trembling. Her perfume, mixed with the scent of fresh grass, clung softly to her.

Gently, he nudged her back and set the fork down on the table.

"You must be tired from the drive," he said, voice calm. "Take a seat."

The smile on Sarah's face faltered for a moment, her eyes dimming just slightly.

But she recovered quickly, her poise returning as she glanced at the meal laid out before her.

"I didn't expect you to cook," she said with a light laugh. "But this looks amazing."

They sat down to eat together. Evander explained how he had prepared each dish. Sarah, in return, described the scenery along her drive. She spoke of vast sunflower fields and roadside stands selling homemade cheese, her voice warm with affection for the countryside.

Evander listened quietly, nodding now and then, and as the sunlight filtered through the windows, the air between them softened. The cool distance from the night before had started to melt, just a little.
