Chapter 36 Journey to Mortis Gorge

Evander bounded downstairs the next morning, his black tracksuit clinging to him like a shadow.

Outside, Ciotti crouched by a beat-up military jeep, running her fingers over the tire treads like she'd done it a hundred times.

The gown was gone. She wore a fitted camo tee, her blonde hair yanked into a high ponytail that bared the line of her neck in the dawn light. Sleeves rolled, forearms tanned and sinewy—she looked like an entirely different woman.

"Morning!" Ciotti called, her voice light with a playful edge.

She stood, brushing dirt off her hands, and jerked her chin toward the stacked gear against the wall. "Give me a hand, will you?"

Evander stepped closer, eyes widening at the arsenal laid out. Military uniforms folded tight beside parachute packs. Tactical belts gleamed with knives and pistols. Black cases cradled rifles and machine guns. Compasses, topo tools, crates of rations and water—all neat, ready.

"Planning for Armageddon?" Evander quipped, picking up a pistol.

Its cold weight sat heavy in his palm, steel glinting with quiet menace.

"Mortis Gorge isn't a Sunday hike," Ciotti shot back, tossing him a vest. "Try that on. If it's off, I'll adjust it. Your grandfather had all this shipped in."

Evander slipped into the vest. Perfect fit.

He watched her move—quick, precise, loading gear into the jeep with practiced efficiency. He flashed back to her vase-shattering kick the night before. She really did have surprises tucked away everywhere.

"Here," Evander said, hefting a topographic case into the trunk with one smooth lift.

Ciotti gave him a quick look, a spark of approval in her eyes.

"Not bad. Guess your girl hasn't run you ragged yet."

Together they packed the jeep, stacking supplies until the trunk and back seat brimmed full.

Evander leaned against the door, catching his breath as sunlight spilled through the palms, warm across his face.

"Thanks," he said quietly.

Ciotti was checking the sights on a rifle. She paused, smiled.

"For what? Helping you chase pirate gold?"

Evander chuckled, the sound loosening the knot in his chest. "For everything. You've done more than I expected."

Her expression softened, though her tone stayed firm.

"Don't get sentimental. This is my mission too. Until we find that elixir, Sir Westbrook's task isn't finished."

Evander nodded. She was right. They were tethered to this quest—sink or swim, together.

They climbed into the jeep. Ciotti slid behind the wheel with the ease of someone born there.

The engine growled, and they rolled out of Azure Cove.

"I'll drive two hours, then you're up," she said, eyes steady on the road.

Evander grunted his agreement, slumping into the passenger seat as the highway blurred by.

The jeep's cabin filled with the low hum of the engine, the steady thump of tires on asphalt.

"It's getting stuffy in here," Ciotti said after a while, her voice cutting through the quiet. "Want to talk?"

Evander turned his head to look at her but said nothing.

Undaunted, Ciotti pressed on, a mischievous glint in her eye.

"So, you and Ms. Nelson—how many rounds did you go on that island? Think she's got a bun in the oven?"

Evander's ears burned. He hadn't seen that coming.

Evander glanced at her. Ciotti's short-sleeved uniform hugged her frame, the open collar revealing a sliver of collarbone, her curves catching the morning light. Her golden lashes shimmered faintly, like threads of sunlight.

"Thirty-seven," he said, his voice taut. "Always inside."

Ciotti's hands tightened on the wheel, her breath catching. She shot him a quick, wide-eyed look.

"Didn't peg you for such a stallion. Ms. Nelson… have a good time?"

Catching the teasing lilt, Evander smirked and leaned into it.

"You trained me into a ninth-dan karate master. Of course I had her seeing stars every time."

Ciotti's laugh burst out, bright and contagious, filling the cabin like sunlight through an open window. Her cheeks flushed, her legs shifting almost imperceptibly.

Evander noticed, but he let his gaze slide back to the blur of the passing road.

She was playing, killing the monotony of the long drive—but the spark she'd lit in him wasn't as easy to shrug off.

"Seriously, though," Ciotti said once her laughter faded, her tone softening. "What's going on with you and Ms. Nelson?"

Evander went quiet, thoughts knotting together.

Kimberly. What was she to him? It had started as nothing but a mission, a calculated move to pass his grandfather's test.

But thirty-seven nights of raw intensity left something behind. A guilt that wouldn't let go—or maybe a pull he couldn't name.

"I don't know," he admitted finally, his voice low. "At first it was just the mission. But after all that time, that… fire—it feels like there's more. Something I can't put into words."

He left it there. Even to himself, the feelings weren't clear.

Ciotti didn't push. She just smiled, a knowing glint in her eyes.

"Feelings are like that. Never add up the way you think they will."

The cabin went quiet again, but the air between them hummed, charged like static before a storm.

Two hours later, they pulled into a service station.

Evander slid behind the wheel as the engine rumbled back to life, Ciotti settling into the passenger seat.

"You look wiped. Get some rest," he said, glancing at her pale face.

Ciotti shook her head, stubborn as ever.

"I'm fine. I'll keep you company."

Her gaze lingered on him, the sunlight sharpening the lines of his jaw, giving him a rugged edge as he focused on the road.

"Did Iris konw?" she asked softly.

Evander's grip tightened on the wheel. His voice dropped. "Yeah. She's just… not talking to me right now."

He and Iris were like ships in a fog—close, but never quite finding each other. He left it at that, and Ciotti didn't pry.

The jeep pressed on, winding through busy cities, rolling countryside, and finally into the jagged arms of the mountains.

The road turned rough, the vehicle jolting as the terrain grew wild.

"We're close," Ciotti said, excitement thrumming in her voice as her gaze fixed on the looming peaks. "That's the mouth of Mortis Gorge."

Evander nodded, hands steady on the wheel. He could feel it—the pull of danger, the weight of the unknown tightening around him like a noose.

By the time the sun dipped below the horizon, they'd reached a dense forest, its shadows swallowing the last scraps of light.

They climbed out. The air was thick with damp earth, sharp with something primal.

"We'll camp here tonight," Ciotti said, popping the trunk to sort through gear. "We hit Mortis Gorge at dawn."

Evander helped, and soon two tents stood ready, their teamwork fluid and wordless.

Night draped the forest, alive with the buzz of insects and the restless whisper of leaves. Ciotti sparked a fire, its glow flickering across their faces.

"Eat," she said, tossing him a compressed biscuit and a bottle of water.

Evander chewed slowly, eyes fixed on the flames. A quiet unease twisted in his chest. Whatever waited in Mortis Gorge, he wasn't ready for it.

Ciotti must've sensed it. She reached over, patting his shoulder.

"Don't worry. I've got you."

Evander met her gaze. Her calm steadied him, anchored him. He let out a faint smile.

"It's good… having you here."

The words hit Ciotti harder than she expected, catching her off guard. For a heartbeat, she wanted to close the space between them, to taste his warmth under the open sky.

But tomorrow loomed too large. They'd need every ounce of strength. So she stayed where she was, letting the firelight dance in the silence between them.
