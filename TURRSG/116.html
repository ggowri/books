Chapter 117 Adrift on the Seine

Sarah had barely finished her invitation when Evander gave a slight shake of his head. The quiet anticipation that had just begun to bloom inside her withered on the spot.

Her fingers instinctively clenched at the hem of her skirt, and the smile on her face turned a little stiff.

As one of France's top models, she was used to being pursued, admired, even adored. This was the first time she had ever invited a man to dinner and been turned down. The rejection hit her harder than she expected, leaving her momentarily off balance.

"I'm sorry, Ms. Grant, I already had dinner on the plane," Evander said, his tone calm and unreadable.

"I was planning to rent a wooden boat for the night. Thought I'd drift down the Seine and let myself go for a while. Could you give me a ride?"

The request dropped like a pebble into the still waters of her disappointment, and the ripples of curiosity quickly overtook her frustration.

She had assumed his rejection would bring about awkward silence and distance. Instead, his invitation, odd as it was, offered her a graceful way to stay close.

And those words, "let myself go", only added to the enigma he already was.

He wasn't like the other men she'd met through these matchmaking setups. He didn't follow the script. There was something unpolished, unpredictable about him—and that made him all the more intriguing.

"Of course," Sarah replied quickly, smoothing her voice into something elegant and composed.

"To be honest, I rarely eat dinner anyway. It's a modeling thing."

She started toward her sports car, heels clicking against the pavement, quietly relieved she hadn't embarrassed herself completely.

If anything, the night had taken an unexpectedly interesting turn.

The car slipped back onto the road, heading toward the Seine.

Inside the cabin, silence stretched between them, filled only by the faint whisper of the air vents.

Sarah stole a glance at Evander through the rearview mirror. He was reclined in the passenger seat, eyes closed, his profile cast in soft shadows as streetlights flickered past.

She wanted to ask him, "Why the Seine? Why tonight? Why this strange need for self-exile?"

But every time the question reached her lips, she swallowed it down. She didn't want to pry. Not yet.

About half an hour later, they pulled up by the riverside.

As Sarah unbuckled her seatbelt, her eyes caught on a small wooden boat moored nearby. It had been painted a pale brown, a little worn but clean and well-kept.

The boat was narrow, just wide enough for one person to lie flat. A blanket had been laid across the base, with a cushion propped near the stern. It looked like a makeshift cradle for a man who needed to disappear for a night.

Evander stepped out first and headed straight for the boat.

An old man stood nearby, dressed in a coarse jacket, likely the boat's owner.

Evander pulled several crisp bills from his wallet and handed them over. The man took the money with a weathered smile, muttering a few words in French that sounded like well wishes.

With a small nod, Evander ducked into the boat.

Sarah got out too, her steps quickening as she followed. When she reached the edge of the water and saw him settling into the boat, her brows furrowed in concern.

"What exactly are you doing?" she asked, eyeing the narrow vessel with unease.

The Seine was calm, sure, but nighttime winds could pick up without warning. A boat that small? One wrong wave and it would tip.

Evander turned slightly to face her. The corners of his lips curled into a faint smile.

"I'm spending the night out here," he said. "Letting the river take me wherever it wants. Just for tonight, I want to let go of everything. Drift freely. Tomorrow morning, wherever I end up, I'll text you."

He held up his phone as if to reassure her.

"That's insane," Sarah said, her voice rising with disbelief.

"What if you fall asleep and the wind picks up? What if something happens and no one's around to help? You can't just throw your safety away like this, not even for some dramatic sense of 'letting go.'"

She was genuinely worried. Sarah'd only known him for a few hours, but the impulse to protect him had already taken root.

Evander watched her, something soft flickering in his eyes.

He sat up, leaning forward, his gaze steady.

"If I've decided to let go, then I have to be willing to face the consequences. If the boat capsizes, maybe it means God hasn't forgiven me. And I'll accept that. But if I wake up safe and sound... that means I've made peace with the past. That I can finally move on."

He paused, his eyes locking onto hers with quiet intensity.

"And in that new life... I'd like a woman like you in it."

Sarah's heart gave a hard thump. Color rushed to her cheeks before she could stop it.

She hadn't expected that. Not from him, and not so suddenly. For a moment, she stood there, caught off guard, unable to say a single word.

The night breeze skimmed across the river, brushing through her hair and scattering her thoughts.

Evander smiled again and then lay back down in the boat, adjusting his position until he looked comfortable.

Above him, the night sky stretched out like a velvet canvas, dotted with stars.

Lights along the riverbank shimmered in the water, dancing with every ripple like scattered diamonds.

"I already added your contact info earlier," he said lightly, eyes still on the sky.

"I'll message you in the morning and let you know where I end up."

Sarah lingered by the shore, watching him. Her worry hadn't vanished, but it was now tangled with something else. Something deeper. More personal.

She told herself not to overthink it. He was a stranger, someone she'd known for less than a day.

And yet, seeing him like this, drifting peacefully in the soft current, she couldn't help but feel the moment was... perfect. But perfect in its own strange way.

"Be careful out there," she said quietly.

"If anything happens, call me. Promise me that."

Evander didn't reply. He just gave a soft "mm" and closed his eyes, his face calm for the first time in days.

The boat rocked gently beneath him, like a lullaby sung in waves. For the first time in weeks, his nerves began to relax.

He'd spent so long in a constant state of tension. From his work on the Decision Matrix Model, to the high-stakes partnership with Royal Tech, to the emotional fallout of saying goodbye to Lois... it had all taken its toll.

But now, adrift on a humble little boat on the Seine, he finally felt free.

Sarah didn't leave right away.

She got back in her car, started the engine, and slowly drove along the river, keeping the drifting boat in view.

Through the window, she watched it bob gently in the current, like a single leaf floating down a stream.

When the road ended and she could go no farther, she parked and climbed a nearby footbridge.

From there, she watched the boat fade slowly into the distance, smaller and smaller beneath the star-speckled sky. She didn't know why Evander needed to do this, or where he would be when the sun came up. But somehow, deep down, she believed he'd be okay.

She pulled out her phone, eyes lingering on his name in her contacts. Her finger hovered over the screen for a long time.

But in the end, she didn't call. She didn't want to interrupt the moment.

Out on the river, Evander drifted into sleep.

The boat rocked gently, humming a lullaby in ripples and wind.

In his dreams, he was back in London. Back at the banquet where he first met Lois. She wore a white gown, her smile glowing as she walked toward him.

He reached for her hand, but the vision dissolved before he could touch her. In its place was the night sky over the Seine, the soft glow of the lights, and the quiet hush that wrapped around everything.

His brows twitched as he turned over in his sleep. But then, slowly, a smile tugged at the corners of his lips, one of peace rather than pain.

The night deepened. The wind cooled.

And the little wooden boat, carrying nothing but a man and his past, floated quietly toward the unknown, like a lone traveler bound for somewhere new.
