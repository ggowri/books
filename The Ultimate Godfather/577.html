Osborn and Abraham finally scrambled to their feet, their faces ghostly pale.

They all but dragged Darren, who was still clutching his mangled arm, as they bolted from the room without so much as a backward glance.

As the door slammed shut, Liz seemed to come out of a daze, her expression caught somewhere between awe and disbelief.

Taking a shaky step forward, she hesitated, then lowered her head and bowed slightly. "Mr. Fitzgerald, I... I was wrong. I didn't recognize your importance, and I said things I shouldn't have. Please forgive me."

For the first time, Liz truly understood what it meant to be in the presence of a true power player.

The real big shot would only wield their power when needed.

Marcus, the undisputed king of Kingston's underworld, wasn't just powerful—he exuded an effortless dominance that didn't need to be stated.

Compared to that, Abraham, with all his hollow bravado, was nothing but a child playing dress-up.

That was the distance—a galaxy—between them.

Liz had spent years looking up to men like Abraham, only to realize now how small-minded and foolish she had been. It stung to admit it, but in every way that mattered, her sister Lila had her beat by a mile.

Liz finally saw herself for what she was—a supporting act in a show she thought she was headlining.

And yet, a small part of her clung to the hope that her actions had been motivated by good intentions, no matter how misguided.

Otherwise, the sister bond would end up nowhere but shattered.

"If not for Lila, you wouldn't even be worth my time," he said flatly, his voice carrying a sting that left Liz rooted to the spot.

Marcus glanced at her briefly, his gaze sharp and cold.

His meaning couldn't have been clearer—her apology meant nothing to him.

Liz felt her stomach churn. To Marcus, she wasn't even on the radar.

The only reason Marcus had even bothered to show up to this dinner was because of Lila.

And yet, it had still turned into an unpleasant spectacle.

Jacqueline stood awkwardly nearby, guilt written all over her face.

She couldn't bring herself to meet Marcus' eyes, the memory of her sharp words earlier cutting into her like a dull knife.

Lila, ever perceptive, stepped in, her voice warm and reassuring. "Mom, you don't need to beat yourself up about this. I know you were just trying to protect me. Maybe not like that next time, okay?"

Jacqueline's eyes filled with tears as she grabbed Lila's hand. "It's all my fault. I shouldn't have been so quick to judge. I hope you can forgive me.

"You may be my stepdaughter, but you're a better daughter than I deserve. I hope you can understand that I meant for your own good.

Lila smiled, shaking her head. "If I didn't know where your heart was, do you think I'd still be here having dinner with you?"

Jacqueline pulled Lila into a tight hug, her tears spilling freely now.

Marcus watched the emotional exchange in silence, his face unreadable. He had no interest in intervening—it wasn't his place to meddle in Lila's family affairs.

After a few more words, Marcus and Lila finally excused themselves, leaving the room together.

Liz stared after them, her regret weighing heavily on her chest.

If only she hadn't been so quick to mock Marcus, a single word from him could've opened doors she hadn't even known existed.

But now? That ship had sailed. With the way she'd treated him, Marcus wouldn't throw her a crumb unless she got on her knees—or begged Lila to do it for her.

"Liz," Jacqueline said softly, her tone filled with regret. "We can't hurt your sister again—not after everything she's been through. She lost her mother young, and she's done so much for us. She still comes back every year, and still helps us when we need it. I don't deserve her kindness."

Liz nodded so hard it was a wonder her neck didn't snap. Regret etched deep lines across her face.

She couldn't believe how she'd treated Lila—her own sister.

Sisters were supposed to stick together, no matter what.
