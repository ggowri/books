At this moment, Grace looked radiant, her complexion glowing with health—a stark contrast to her previous pallor and frailty. She now appeared as vibrant and healthy as she once had been, exuding a fresh and captivating charm.

Marcus almost did not recognize her at first glance.

"Mr. Fitzgerald, thank you for saving my life!" Grace bowed deeply, her delicate face filled with heartfelt gratitude.

For years, she had endured relentless suffering from her illness, burdening Benjamin and Lilian with worry and living in the shadow of despair.

Grace had resigned herself to the belief that her life was nearing its end.

Then, at her darkest hour, Marcus had appeared, using his extraordinary skills to cure her leukemia.

To Grace, it felt like a rebirth, a miracle beyond words.

Marcus helped Grace up, smiling modestly. "It was nothing, Ms. Mitchell. No need to thank me."

"Mayor Benjamin, do you have guests?"

A voice interrupted them, speaking Uswadic with a noticeable foreign accent.

The awkward pronunciation immediately suggested the speaker was not Uswadian.

"Mr. Griffin, let me introduce you. This is Mr. Fitzgerald, my daughter Grace's savior and one of Solomia's most outstanding young talents."

Four figures entered the room—three men and one woman.

Among them were two middle-aged men in their fifties, a young man in his twenties, and a teenage girl.

Benjamin eagerly introduced Marcus to the group.

The four visitors scrutinized Marcus with polite yet reserved expressions.

"Mr. Fitzgerald, it is a pleasure to meet you. I am Terry Griffin," Terry said, stepping forward to shake hands.

He gestured toward the others. "This is my younger brother, Zane Griffin, my son, Easton Griffin, and my daughter, Charlotte Griffin."

Marcus exchanged greetings with them, though he could not shake a lingering discomfort. Historical grievances had left him with a deep-seated aversion to people from Crankhurst.

During the introductions, Terry's gaze lingered on Grace with a predatory intensity.

Though he tried to conceal it, Marcus' sharp eyes caught the fleeting expression.

Meanwhile, Lilian had prepared a table full of traditional Uswadian dishes, each one beautifully presented and bursting with flavor. The spread rivaled any five-star restaurant, yet carried the warmth of a home-cooked meal.

"Mr. Griffin, since this is your first visit to Uswad, please enjoy some of our cuisine and compare it to Crankhurst's," Benjamin said, gesturing for Terry to begin.

Terry smiled, "Uswadic culture is vast and profound. I have always admired it from afar. Seeing it firsthand, especially through its cuisine, I can tell it is incredibly diverse."

Zane sampled a dish, his expression turning peculiar. "Uswadic food is flavorful, but I find it masks the natural taste of the ingredients. Compared to Crankhurst's culinary traditions, it is somewhat lacking.

"I have also noticed that much of Uswadic culture seems borrowed from Crankhurst. For a country with a supposed 5,000-year history, it is disappointing."

"I have been meaning to say the same thing," Charlotte chimed in. "From what I have studied, during the wars decades ago, Uswad was defeated by Crankhurst. Even now, in terms of culture, economy, history, and medicine, Uswad lags far behind us. I do not understand why my dad insists on doing business with such a country."

Charlotte's words were laced with condescension, and she delivered them in halting Uswadic, making no secret of her disdain for Uswad.

More importantly, the things she and Zane said were completely turning the truth upside down!

Her blatant insults and revisionist remarks darkened the expressions of Benjamin and Grace.

They thought, "Earlier, the Crankhurstians had seemed courteous and respectful. But now that the business deal is sealed, their true arrogance is on full display."

"Mayor Benjamin mentioned that this man saved Ms. Mitchell from leukemia. In an advanced country like Crankhurst, even we struggle to treat such diseases.

"Given Uswad's outdated medical system, it is highly likely she was misdiagnosed. Your doctor probably mistook a minor blood condition for leukemia. Otherwise, how could someone so young and inexperienced cure such a serious illness?"

Charlotte's voice dripped with scorn.

Though she looked no older than eighteen or nineteen, her arrogance was palpable, as if Crankhurst were the pinnacle of civilization in her mind.
